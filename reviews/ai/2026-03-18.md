### Atmospheric Context

March 18 was Day 2 of a post-frontal continental regime at KMIA. The synoptic setup was a continuation of 3/17: high pressure centered to the north/northwest, persistent NNW surface flow advecting modified continental air across South Florida. The 850 mb temperatures around 10-11C and 925 mb temps around 13-14C confirmed a cool mid-level air mass.

CAPE was generally near zero through the overnight and early morning hours. Some models showed brief CAPE development (50-80 J/kg) during the warm intrusion window around 09-11 LST, but this was never enough to generate convection. Precipitable water was in the 28-37 mm range — well below tropical values but not bone-dry either.

The key atmospheric feature was the **brief warm intrusion at 09-11 LST**. The surface obs show a clear wind shift from NNW (330-360 degrees) to briefly N/NE (010 degrees) at 11 LST, with the temperature spike corresponding to this rotation. The most likely explanation is a brief mesoscale relaxation of the post-frontal northerly flow, allowing residual warmth from the marine boundary layer to the south to intrude briefly. The rapid return of NNW flow by 11:48 LST confirms this was a transient feature, not a regime change.

Cloud cover remained at 100% all day. This mattered in two ways: (1) it limited solar heating during the warm pulse, preventing the high from reaching even higher, and (2) it slowed radiative cooling in the evening, explaining why the temperature plateaued at 64.4F rather than grinding lower like on 3/17.

The most important difference is in the **timing of the extremes**. On 3/17, the high was set in the opening hour and the low was set at the end — a classic cold grind. On 3/18, the low was set in the opening hour and the high required a mid-morning event — an inverted structure where both extremes were inherited from or generated by transient phenomena rather than sustained trends.

### Signal-to-Adjustment Notes

#### High-side adjustment labels from this case

- **`warm_intrusion_attempt`**
  - Trigger: wind shift from NNW to briefly SSW/SE during a post-frontal regime
  - Case evidence: 59F to 69.8F in 2 hours with dew point spike
  - Use later as: temporarily widen sigma on the high side when a wind shift is detected, but do not shift mu until the intrusion persists for >3 hours

- **`warm_intrusion_rejected`**
  - Trigger: NNW flow returns within 2 hours of a warm intrusion, temperature drops >5F/hr
  - Case evidence: 69.8F to 64.4F in <1 hour once NNW returned
  - Use later as: clamp high-side distribution at the intrusion peak; the regime has been tested and survived

- **`high_capped_by_regime`**
  - Trigger: afternoon temps cannot approach the morning warm-intrusion peak
  - Case evidence: 68F ceiling in the afternoon vs 69.8F morning peak
  - Use later as: confirm that the high is settled and remove any remaining warm-tail probability

#### Low-side adjustment labels from this case

- **`low_locked_early`**
  - Trigger: overnight cold floor from prior day carries forward into the opening hours of the new climate day
  - Case evidence: 55.4F obs low at 00:00-01:00 AM LST
  - Use later as: when the low is set in the opening hours during a multi-day cold event, the low-side distribution is effectively solved early

- **`postfrontal_persistence_day2`**
  - Trigger: post-frontal NNW flow persists into a second climate day
  - Case evidence: same regime, same low (56F both days)
  - Use later as: on Day 2+ of a post-frontal event, the cold-floor distribution from Day 1 should be the starting prior for Day 2

### Best Trade Lessons from the Day

#### Best high-side idea

- **The high-side edge on 3/18 was knowing that most models were 2-3F too warm**
- ECMWF at 71.6F, KNMI/MetNo at 72.1F, NAM-MOS at 72F — all systematically over-forecasting the warming potential
- GEM-Global at 69.1F was the right read
- The correct expression would have been selling warm high-side brackets

#### Best low-side idea

- **The low was set in the opening hours — low-side trades needed to be placed pre-dawn or inherited from 3/17 positions**
- Unlike 3/17, there was no midday opportunity to enter low-side trades at good prices because the low was already locked
- The carry-forward from 3/17 positions was the real edge

#### Biggest structural mistake to avoid

- **Treating the 09-11 LST warm intrusion as a regime break**
- In real time, the wind shift and 10F warming looked like the post-frontal regime might be ending
- The correct read was to wait for the intrusion to prove itself (persist >3 hours) before adjusting the distribution
- Models that assumed sustained warming were the worst performers

### Data Caveats

- CLI remains the settlement truth.
- Obs are used to understand path, timing, and signals.
- The model consensus table contains many duplicate entries from different run times for the same model. Weight analysis should freshness-filter to avoid over-counting stale runs.
- The wethr source models (NBM, LAV-MOS, NAM, RAP) show dramatically different values from their openmeteo counterparts for the same model family. This suggests the wethr pipeline may be returning older or differently-processed runs.
- Some RAP entries show 49.4F for the low, which appears anomalous and may represent a different forecast element or a data ingestion error.
- The 100% cloud cover all day is notable — on a clear post-frontal day, the overnight low would likely have been colder and the daytime high potentially warmer. The overcast modulated both extremes.

## Bottom Line

March 18 should be remembered as a **Day 2 post-frontal persistence day with a failed warm intrusion**.

That gives three durable lessons:

1. **Post-frontal cold regimes at KMIA can persist for multiple days.** The same NNW flow, the same cold floor (56F both days), and the same model failures repeated on Day 2. Multi-day regime persistence is not noise — it is a predictable feature.
2. **A brief warm intrusion that reverses in under 2 hours is regime confirmation, not regime destruction.** The 10F warming at 09-11 LST looked dramatic, but the rapid NNW return proved the continental air mass was dominant. A tested-and-survived regime is stronger, not weaker.
3. **When the low is set in the opening hours of a multi-day cold event, the market opportunity structure is fundamentally different from a grind-down day.** On 3/17, low-side trades could be entered at midday. On 3/18, the low was already locked — the edge was in carrying positions from the prior day, not in intraday entry.
