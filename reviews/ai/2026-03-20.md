**Executive Summary**

March 20 was a messy but interpretable KMIA transition day. It opened like a continuation of the prior cool-background regime, threw a false morning breakout around 09:20 LST, then rebuilt into the real afternoon solution: a sustained rebound that ultimately settled HIGH 78 / LOW 61. Promotion stance: **hold-for-more-data** — the stats step tried to update `mixed_uncertain`, but that is not the right regime bucket for what actually happened.

**Ground Truth**

CLI settlement is the only truth: **HIGH 78F at 3:14 PM LST** and **LOW 61F at 7:04 AM LST**. Sampled station obs support that structure closely even if they miss the exact terminal print: KMIA sampled **60.8F** repeatedly in the pre-dawn window and sampled **77.0F** around **13:25 LST** and again near **15:10-15:15 LST**. Those sampled obs are useful for path analysis, but the settled values are 78/61.

**Regime Assessment**

Four regimes were active.

1. **`overnight_residual_cooling`** from midnight through roughly 08 LST. This looked like a softened continuation of the prior cool regime: temperatures bled down from 64.4F to 60.8F under 310-350 degree flow, dew points eased into the upper-50s, and pressure recovered into sunrise. That is a classic low-side solve window.
2. **`brief_warm_intrusion_failure`** from about 08:30 to 10:55 LST. The station surged from 62.6F to 71.6F as winds backed toward the northeast/east, but the move did not hold. By 10:55 LST KMIA was back to 66.2F. That was a real pulse, but it failed the persistence test.
3. **`secondary_rebound_with_afternoon_high_lock`** from late morning through mid-afternoon. This is the new regime worth naming. After the first pulse failed, the day re-accelerated in a broader, more durable way and then parked in the 75-77F neighborhood for hours. That second rebound, not the first pulse, was the true high-side settlement engine.
4. **`evening_cooling_reversion`** after 16 LST. Once the afternoon shelf ended, the day cooled cleanly back into the upper-60s and mid-60s with no late warm run.

The most important classifier takeaway is the SKF misclassification issue. The pre-review statistical step updated **`mixed_uncertain`**, but this was not a generic mixed day. The morning phase specifically matches **`brief_warm_intrusion_failure`**, and the winning midday/afternoon phase matches **`secondary_rebound_with_afternoon_high_lock`**. If the live SKF treats this as mixed noise, it will learn the wrong lesson.

**Phase Breakdown**

From midnight to about 07 LST, KMIA behaved like a residual cool-floor case. Temperatures moved from the mid-60s into the lower-60s, with repeated 60.8F sampled obs before sunrise. The low-side market was effectively solved once it became clear the station would not recover before dawn.

From about 08:30 to 10:00 LST, the day tried to break out early. KMIA climbed rapidly through 64.4F, 66.2F, 68F, 69.8F, and 71.6F as winds backed and skies briefly cleared. If that move had persisted, this would have looked like a straightforward rebound continuation of 3/19.

But the first breakout failed. By 10:55 LST the station had fallen back to 66.2F, with cloud cover back up and dew points temporarily elevated. That is the exact place where a weak classifier can overfit the first pulse and call the day solved too early.

The real signal came after 11 LST. KMIA recovered again into 69.8F, 71.6F, 73.4F, 73.9F, and then spent hours mainly between 73.4F and 77.0F. That broader recovery arc is what distinguishes today from 3/18. On 3/18 the warm pulse failed and the day never truly reassembled. On 3/20 the day *did* reassemble, and that second leg carried the settlement high.

From roughly 13 LST through 16 LST, the day was basically an afternoon shelf. Sampled obs kept revisiting 75-77F, and CLI finally settled the high at 78F at 15:14 LST. Once that shelf existed, the hot tail should have been pruned aggressively.

After 16 LST, the structure simplified again. KMIA cooled from 75.2F to 73.4F, 71.6F, 69.8F, 68.0F, and 66.2F by late evening. No late re-heating occurred.

**Signal-to-Adjustment Notes**

- **`low_locked_early`** should fire when the sunrise floor is already visible in the pre-dawn data and the overnight background stays northerly or dry enough that no warmer reset is coming before settlement.
- **`high_probe_up_then_verify`** is the correct label for an initial warm pulse that looks interesting but has not yet passed the persistence test. This should widen attention, not immediately shift the whole day into a warm regime.
- **`warm_pulse_rejected`** matters because it prevents the system from over-learning off the first spike. A rejected pulse is evidence about regime resilience.
- **`high_mu_shift_up_small`** belongs to the *second* rebound on this day, not the first. The adjustment should wait for the broader leg that actually carried settlement.
- **`warm_tail_removed_after_lock`** should trigger once the high neighborhood is established and multiple subsequent observations fail to extend it materially.
- **`no_late_heat_run`** is the clean confirmation label that seals the afternoon high as terminal.

**Model Performance**

The best balanced latest model was **GEM-Global (openmeteo)** at **75.5 / 60.8**, only **-2.5F** on the high and **-0.2F** on the low. **ICON-Global** and **UKMO-Global** were also respectable two-sided performers. **KNMI-Harmonie** and **MetNo-Nordic** deserve credit for nailing the low at **61.0F**, even though both were too cool on the high.

The openmeteo family, broadly speaking, understood the overnight floor much better than the afternoon rebound. Most of them clustered in the **74-75F** range on the high, which is directionally fine but still too cool by **2.5-4F**. Their consensus error aligns with the stats step: the high-side consensus undercooked the actual day by about **3.7F**.

The wethr family remained structurally worse. Many members were **too warm on the low and too cool on the high at the same time**, which is exactly the wrong error shape for this day. **HRRR (wethr)**, **NAM4KM**, **RAP**, and **ICON (wethr)** all badly missed the afternoon rebound, while **UKMO**, **GFS**, **NBM**, and **LAV-MOS** badly missed the sunrise low. That still looks more like stale or differently processed guidance than a coherent meteorological signal.

**Spatial Confirmation**

The surrounding network supports the story. **KOPF** ran **77/60**, very close in structure to KMIA. **KHWO** reached **80F**, while **CHKF1** and **LPIF1** reached **79F**, confirming that the afternoon rebound into the upper-70s was regionally real. On the cool side, inland sites like **CHKF1**, **KTMB**, and **LPIF1** dropped into the mid/upper-50s, showing the dawn cool floor was also real and not a bad KMIA print.

FAWN Homestead is especially useful here: it bottomed at **59.3F around 06:45 LST** and then climbed to **77.2F around 13:30 LST**. That is the same broad daily arc as KMIA, just slightly cooler at dawn and similarly capped in the upper-70s by afternoon.

**Ocean Influence**

The marine background was supportive but not explosively warm. **Virginia Key** water temperatures ran about **72.7-75.0F**, **Hollywood Beach buoy** about **75.9-76.8F**, and **Manatee Bay** about **69.6-73.4F**. Those values help explain why the morning pulse could happen and why the day eventually recovered, but they also argue against a true tropical overrun into the 80s. The ocean signal supported moderation and rebound, not runaway heat.

**Upper Air Analysis**

Low-level thermal fields still looked only modestly warm. Pressure-level data showed **925 mb temperatures mostly around 14-15C** and **850 mb temperatures generally around 8-11C**. Overnight and early-morning winds at those levels remained broadly northerly to northeasterly, which fits the residual cool-floor regime. The afternoon surface rebound was therefore more about boundary-layer recovery and mesoscale coastal/inland adjustment than a deep tropical reset aloft.

**Market Timing**

The low-side market solved earliest. Once the pre-dawn floor persisted into sunrise and CLI eventually settled 61F at 07:04 LST, there was little reason to keep a wide low-side distribution alive.

The harder read was on the high side. The biggest mistake would have been treating the **first** morning breakout as the final answer. The better trade was recognizing that the 09:20 pulse was only a probe, then waiting for the **second**, broader rebound and the afternoon shelf to confirm that the day had genuinely transitioned into a high-side recovery path. After the shelf formed, warm-tail contracts should have been pruned aggressively rather than chased.

**Bottom Line**

1. **This was not a pure continuation day and not a pure failure day.** It was a two-step warm-rebound case: first pulse failed, second pulse won.
2. **SKF should not learn this as `mixed_uncertain`.** The correct labels are more specific: `brief_warm_intrusion_failure` in the morning, then `secondary_rebound_with_afternoon_high_lock` into settlement.
3. **Promotion stance is hold-for-more-data.** The trained candidate can absorb today’s new labels, but the live configs should not be updated from a misbucketed statistical proposal.
