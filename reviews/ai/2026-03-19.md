**Executive Summary**

March 19 was a genuine transition day at KMIA: the low side still inherited some residual cool-regime structure from the prior post-frontal stretch, but the bulk of the climate day behaved like a normal spring rebound with a clean midday high lock. HIGH and LOW need to be scored separately here: the LOW was effectively solved near sunrise, while the HIGH required a sustained 5-hour warming ramp into early afternoon.

**Ground Truth**

CLI settlement is the truth: **HIGH 78F at 1:03 PM LST** and **LOW 62F at 7:02 AM LST**. Surface obs align closely with that settlement structure: the sampled obs floor was **62.1F at 05:53 LST** under 340-degree flow, while the sampled obs peak was **78.8F around 12:40-13:00 LST** with northeast flow and dew points near 55.4F. Those obs are for path/timing interpretation only; the settled market truth is 78/62.

**Regime Assessment**

Three regimes were active. First, **`overnight_residual_cooling`** carried over from the 3/17-3/18 post-frontal pattern. Northerly flow, a cooler pre-dawn floor, and sub-66F temperatures through sunrise all support that classification. Second, the day transitioned into **`daytime_rebound_with_midday_high_lock`**. This was not the short, unstable warm intrusion pattern from 3/18. Instead, the warming lasted from roughly 08 LST through early afternoon and moved steadily rather than in one fragile burst. Third, **`evening_cooling_reversion`** took over after the high printed. The evening curve was orderly, with no secondary heat run and a clean fade back into the mid-60s.

**Phase Breakdown**

From midnight through about 07 LST, KMIA held mostly between 62F and 66F. The pre-dawn environment was drier than the prior day, with dew points settling from the low/mid-60s toward the upper-50s and winds generally north to north-northeast. That allowed the low side to resolve early.

From about 07 to 13 LST, the structure flipped. KMIA climbed from the sunrise floor through 64.4F, 69.8F, 73.4F, then into the upper-70s. This was not a single spike; it was a broad warming ramp. The longer duration is the key distinction from 3/18's failed warm intrusion. By the time KMIA reached the 77-79F zone near midday, the day had clearly become a rebound case rather than another cold-grind continuation.

From 13 to 18 LST, the market had a classic high-lock environment. Temperatures oscillated mostly between 75F and 77F after the 78.8F obs peak, which is exactly the sort of plateau that should collapse warm-tail probability. There was no credible second acceleration toward 80F or higher.

From 18 LST to the end of the climate day, cooling resumed steadily: roughly low-70s into upper-60s and then back to 64.4F by late evening. That late cooling did not matter for settlement, but it strongly confirmed that the midday peak was terminal.

**Signal-to-Adjustment Notes**

- **`low_locked_early`** should fire when the climate-day floor is effectively found near sunrise under persistent northerly or residual continental flow. This label is low-side only.
- **`high_mu_shift_up_small`** is appropriate when warming is sustained across multiple hours, not when there is only a 60-90 minute pulse. This label is high-side only.
- **`warm_tail_removed_after_lock`** should activate once the observed high has printed and 1-2 hours of follow-through fail to produce a new peak.
- **`no_late_heat_run`** is the clean confirmation label when evening cooling begins on time and there is no second warm surge.

**Model Performance**

The best balanced model was **JMA-GSM (openmeteo)**, which came in only -1.4F on the HIGH and -0.7F on the LOW. **KNMI-Harmonie / MetNo-Nordic** were also solid with mild cool bias on both sides. **GEM-Global** deserves credit for exactly nailing the LOW at 62F, but it materially undercooked the HIGH at 72.2F, so its success was one-sided.

The weakest performers were mostly warm on the LOW or much too cool on the HIGH. **GFS-GraphCast** had a decent HIGH but missed the LOW by +4.6F. **HRRR (wethr)** was a major failure, missing the HIGH by -12.3F and the LOW by +2.5F, which still looks more like a data freshness or processing mismatch than a true forecast signal.

**Spatial Confirmation**

Nearby stations support the transition-day interpretation. Inland and more heat-responsive sites such as **KTMB**, **KHWO**, **CHKF1**, and **LPIF1** reached roughly **79-80F**, while cooler coastal/northern sites like **KFXE** and **KFLL** stayed more muted, mostly **73-75F** for the high. **KOPF** tracked KMIA closely with a 77/61 profile. That spatial pattern argues for real daytime mixing and inland heat support, not a random bad observation at KMIA.

FAWN Homestead also backs this up: **59.3F low near 06:45 LST** and **77.2F high near 13:30 LST**. That is a stronger early-morning cool floor than KMIA but a very similar daytime recovery arc.

**Ocean Influence**

The marine background was not hot enough to explain the day by itself. **Virginia Key** water temperatures were roughly **72.9-75.4F**, **Hollywood Beach buoy** sat around **75.4-76.5F**, and **Manatee Bay** ranged about **70.0-73.2F**. Those values are supportive of coastal moderation, but they do not create a tropical warm surge. Instead, the ocean signal looks like a limiting influence that kept the rebound from extending much beyond the upper-70s.

**Upper Air Analysis**

The low-level airmass remained fundamentally northerly. Pressure-level data showed **925 mb temperatures roughly 13-16C** and **850 mb temperatures around 10-12C**, with wind directions at both levels generally from the **north to northeast** through the full climate day. That is consistent with a modified but still continental low-level source region. In other words, the column was not signaling a full tropical reset; it was signaling a spring recovery day inside a still-cooler background regime.

**Market Timing**

The low-side market solved quickly. By about **09:30 LST**, the winning **LOW 61.0-61.5** bracket was already trading around **95-97c**, and by around **11:00 LST** it was essentially a **98-99c** instrument. There was little remaining low-side edge after the sunrise floor was confirmed.

The better intraday opportunity was on the HIGH side. The winning **HIGH 77.0-77.5** bracket traded around the low-50s during the morning rebound, climbed into the mid/high-80s by roughly **12:30 LST**, then eventually resolved into the upper-90s later in the day. The key trade was recognizing that the day was a sustained rebound rather than another failed warm pulse.

**Bottom Line**

1. **This was not another 3/18.** The warming lasted too long and was too orderly to classify as a brief failed intrusion.
2. **LOW and HIGH had very different solution windows.** The LOW was mostly solved near sunrise; the HIGH required patience until the morning rebound proved durable.
3. **Midday high lock matters.** Once the high printed and the afternoon flattened, the warm tail should have been pruned aggressively. That is the durable lesson this case adds to the training set.
